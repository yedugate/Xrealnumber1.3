Euclid’s Division Lemma

Class 9 CBSE Mathematics — Real Numbers

1. Introduction

Euclid was a Greek mathematician who made important contributions to geometry and number theory. One famous result connected with his name is Euclid’s Division Lemma.

The word lemma means a proven statement that is used to prove another mathematical result.

Euclid’s Division Lemma is a very important concept in Class 9 because it forms the base for understanding:

  • Division of integers
  • Remainder concept
  • Even and odd numbers
  • Number forms such as $3q$, $3q+1$, $3q+2$
  • HCF using Euclid’s Division Algorithm

2. Statement of Euclid’s Division Lemma

For any two positive integers $a$ and $b$, there exist unique integers $q$ and $r$ such that:

$$ a = bq + r $$

where:

$$ 0 \leq r < b $$

Here:

Symbol Meaning
$a$ Dividend, the number being divided
$b$ Divisor, the number by which we divide
$q$ Quotient
$r$ Remainder

In simple words:

$$ \text{Dividend} = \text{Divisor} \times \text{Quotient} + \text{Remainder} $$

3. Basic Level Understanding

3.1 Meaning of division

Division means splitting a number into equal groups.

Example:

You have 25 chocolates and 7 students.

Each student can get 3 chocolates.

$$ 7 \times 3 = 21 $$

Remaining chocolates:

$$ 25 - 21 = 4 $$

So:

$$ 25 = 7 \times 3 + 4 $$

Here:

Term Value
Dividend 25
Divisor 7
Quotient 3
Remainder 4

This is Euclid’s Division Lemma.

3.2 Example from the textbook: Dividing 58 by 9

When 58 is divided by 9:

$$ 58 = 9 \times 6 + 4 $$

Here:

Term Value
$a$ 58
$b$ 9
$q$ 6
$r$ 4

Check the condition:

$$ 0 \leq r < b $$

$$ 0 \leq 4 < 9 $$

So the division form is correct.

4. Why should the remainder be less than the divisor?

The condition:

$$ 0 \leq r < b $$

is very important.

It means:

  1. The remainder should be zero or positive.
  2. The remainder should be smaller than the divisor.

Example:

$$ 25 = 7 \times 3 + 4 $$

Here the divisor is 7 and the remainder is 4.

$$ 4 < 7 $$

So this is correct.

But suppose we write:

$$ 25 = 7 \times 2 + 11 $$

This is mathematically equal because:

$$ 7 \times 2 + 11 = 14 + 11 = 25 $$

But this is not the correct Euclid division form, because the remainder is 11.

$$ 11 > 7 $$

The remainder should not be greater than or equal to the divisor. If 11 is left, we can still divide it by 7 one more time. So the correct form is:

$$ 25 = 7 \times 3 + 4 $$

5.1 Case 1: Remainder is zero

If one number is exactly divisible by another, the remainder is zero.

Example:

$$ 35 = 5 \times 7 + 0 $$

Here:

$$ q = 7,\quad r = 0 $$

Check:

$$ 0 \leq 0 < 5 $$

So this is correct.

5.2 Case 2: Divisor is greater than dividend

Example:

$$ 7 = 15 \times 0 + 7 $$

Here, 15 cannot go into 7 even once.

So:

$$ q = 0,\quad r = 7 $$

Check:

$$ 0 \leq 7 < 15 $$

So Euclid’s Division Lemma works even when the divisor is greater than the dividend.

6. Real-life understanding

6.1 Chocolate sharing example

Suppose you have 58 chocolates and 9 students.

Each student can get 6 chocolates.

$$ 9 \times 6 = 54 $$

Remaining chocolates:

$$ 58 - 54 = 4 $$

So:

$$ 58 = 9 \times 6 + 4 $$

Situation Mathematical term
Total chocolates Dividend
Number of students Divisor
Chocolates each student gets Quotient
Chocolates left Remainder

So Euclid’s Division Lemma is simply the mathematical form of equal sharing.

6.2 Bus seating example

Suppose 47 students are going on a trip. Each bus can carry 10 students.

Number of full buses:

$$ 10 \times 4 = 40 $$

Remaining students:

$$ 47 - 40 = 7 $$

So:

$$ 47 = 10 \times 4 + 7 $$

Here:

Term Value
Dividend 47 students
Divisor 10 seats per bus
Quotient 4 full buses
Remainder 7 students left

In real life, one extra bus may be needed for the remaining 7 students, but mathematically the Euclid division form is:

$$ 47 = 10 \times 4 + 7 $$

6.3 Packing apples example

A shopkeeper has 83 apples. He packs them in boxes of 12 apples each.

$$ 12 \times 6 = 72 $$

Remaining apples:

$$ 83 - 72 = 11 $$

So:

$$ 83 = 12 \times 6 + 11 $$

Check:

$$ 0 \leq 11 < 12 $$

So the division form is correct.

7. Intermediate Level Understanding

7.1 Possible remainders

When a number is divided by $b$, possible remainders are:

$$ 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots, b-1 $$

That means the remainder can start from 0, but it must stop one number before the divisor.

7.2 Possible remainders when divided by 2

When a number is divided by 2, possible remainders are:

$$ 0, 1 $$

So every positive integer can be written as:

$$ 2q $$

or

$$ 2q + 1 $$

If the remainder is 0, the number is even.

$$ a = 2q $$

If the remainder is 1, the number is odd.

$$ a = 2q + 1 $$

Examples:

Number Form Type
8 $2 \times 4 + 0$ Even
9 $2 \times 4 + 1$ Odd
14 $2 \times 7 + 0$ Even
15 $2 \times 7 + 1$ Odd

7.3 Possible remainders when divided by 3

When a number is divided by 3, possible remainders are:

$$ 0, 1, 2 $$

So any positive integer can be written as:

$$ 3q,\quad 3q+1,\quad 3q+2 $$

Examples:

Number Euclid form
12 $3 \times 4 + 0 = 3q$
13 $3 \times 4 + 1 = 3q+1$
14 $3 \times 4 + 2 = 3q+2$
15 $3 \times 5 + 0 = 3q$

7.4 Possible remainders when divided by 4

When a number is divided by 4, possible remainders are:

$$ 0, 1, 2, 3 $$

So any positive integer can be written as:

$$ 4q,\quad 4q+1,\quad 4q+2,\quad 4q+3 $$

Examples:

Number Euclid form

8. Advanced Level Understanding

8.1 Meaning of uniqueness

Euclid’s Division Lemma says that $q$ and $r$ are unique.

Unique means that for a fixed $a$ and $b$, there is only one correct quotient and one correct remainder satisfying:

$$ a = bq + r $$

where:

$$ 0 \leq r < b $$

Example:

$$ 58 = 9 \times 6 + 4 $$

Can we write?

$$ 58 = 9 \times 5 + 13 $$

Numerically:

$$ 9 \times 5 + 13 = 45 + 13 = 58 $$

But this is not valid because:

$$ 13 > 9 $$

Can we write?

$$ 58 = 9 \times 7 - 5 $$

Numerically:

$$ 63 - 5 = 58 $$

But this is not valid because the remainder cannot be negative.

So only this form is valid:

$$ 58 = 9 \times 6 + 4 $$

Therefore, quotient and remainder are unique.

8.2 Proof idea of uniqueness

Suppose there are two different representations:

$$ a = bq + r $$

and:

$$ a = bs + t $$

where:

$$ 0 \leq r < b $$

and:

$$ 0 \leq t < b $$

Since both are equal to $a$:

$$ bq + r = bs + t $$

Rearranging:

$$ r - t = bs - bq $$

$$ r - t = b(s-q) $$

This means $r-t$ is a multiple of $b$.

But both $r$ and (t) are smaller than $b$. So the difference between $r$ and (t) must be less than $b$.

The only multiple of $b$ between (-b) and $b$ is 0.

Therefore:

$$ r - t = 0 $$

So:

$$ r = t $$

Then:

$$ bq = bs $$

So:

$$ q = s $$

Therefore, quotient and remainder are unique.

8.3 Euclid’s Division Lemma and arithmetic progression

In the textbook proof, we consider the multiples of $b$:

$$ \ldots, -3b, -2b, -b, 0, b, 2b, 3b, \ldots $$

These numbers form an arithmetic progression with common difference $b$.

For any positive integer $a$, we find the largest multiple of $b$ that is less than or equal to $a$.

That multiple is:

$$ bq $$

Then the remaining part is:

$$ r = a - bq $$

So:

$$ a = bq + r $$

Since $bq$ is the largest multiple of $b$ not exceeding $a$, the remainder $r$ must be smaller than $b$.

Therefore:

$$ 0 \leq r < b $$

9. Advanced Applications

9.1 Proving the square of every odd integer is of the form $4q+1$

Any odd integer can be written as:

$$ 2m + 1 $$

Now square it:

$$ (2m+1)^2 $$

$$ = 4m^2 + 4m + 1 $$

$$ = 4(m^2+m) + 1 $$

Let:

$$ q = m^2 + m $$

Therefore:

$$ (2m+1)^2 = 4q + 1 $$

Hence, the square of every odd integer is of the form:

$$ 4q + 1 $$

9.2 Proving the square of any integer is of the form $3q$ or $3q+1$

Let $a$ be any positive integer.

By Euclid’s Division Lemma, when $a$ is divided by 3:

$$ a = 3q + r $$

where:

$$ r = 0, 1, 2 $$

Case 1: (a = 3q)

$$ a^2 = $3q$^2 = 9q^2 $$

$$ a^2 = 3(3q^2) $$

So $a^2$ is of the form $3q$.

Case 2: (a = 3q+1)

$$ a^2 = $3q+1$^2 $$

$$ = 9q^2 + 6q + 1 $$

$$ = 3(3q^2 + 2q) + 1 $$

So $a^2$ is of the form $3q+1$.

Case 3: (a = 3q+2)

$$ a^2 = $3q+2$^2 $$

$$ = 9q^2 + 12q + 4 $$

$$ = 9q^2 + 12q + 3 + 1 $$

$$ = 3(3q^2 + 4q + 1) + 1 $$

So $a^2$ is also of the form $3q+1$.

Therefore, the square of any positive integer is of the form:

$$ 3q $$

or:

$$ 3q+1 $$

9.3 Finding HCF using Euclid’s Division Algorithm

Euclid’s Division Lemma is used to find HCF.

Example: Find HCF of 135 and 225

Take the bigger number first.

$$ 225 = 135 \times 1 + 90 $$

Now divide 135 by 90:

$$ 135 = 90 \times 1 + 45 $$

Now divide 90 by 45:

$$ 90 = 45 \times 2 + 0 $$

When the remainder becomes 0, the divisor at that step is the HCF.

So:

$$ \text{HCF} = 45 $$

10. Common mistakes students make

Mistake 1: Taking remainder greater than divisor

Wrong:

$$ 30 = 7 \times 3 + 9 $$

Here remainder is 9 and divisor is 7.

$$ 9 > 7 $$

So it is wrong.

Correct:

$$ 30 = 7 \times 4 + 2 $$

Mistake 2: Taking negative remainder

Wrong:

$$ 30 = 7 \times 5 - 5 $$

Remainder cannot be negative.

Correct:

$$ 30 = 7 \times 4 + 2 $$

Mistake 3: Confusing $a$ and $b$

If the question says, “Apply Euclid’s Division Lemma to 47 and 6,” usually:

$$ a = 47,\quad b = 6 $$

So:

$$ 47 = 6 \times 7 + 5 $$

Do not write:

$$ 6 = 47 \times 0 + 6 $$

unless the question specifically asks to divide 6 by 47.

15. Exam-ready answers

Question 1: State Euclid’s Division Lemma.

Euclid’s Division Lemma states that for any two positive integers $a$ and $b$, there exist unique integers $q$ and $r$ such that:

$$ a = bq + r $$

where:

$$ 0 \leq r < b $$

Here, $q$ is the quotient and $r$ is the remainder.

Question 2: Apply Euclid’s Division Lemma to 58 and 9.

$$ 58 = 9 \times 6 + 4 $$

So:

$$ q = 6,\quad r = 4 $$

Since:

$$ 0 \leq 4 < 9 $$

the division form is valid.

Question 3: What are the possible remainders when divided by 5?

When a number is divided by 5, the remainder must be less than 5.

So the possible remainders are:

$$ 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 $$

Question 4: Why is (58 = 9 \times 5 + 13) not valid according to Euclid’s Division Lemma?

Although:

$$ 9 \times 5 + 13 = 58 $$

it is not valid because the remainder is 13.

Here the divisor is 9.

$$ 13 > 9 $$

But the remainder must be smaller than the divisor.

Correct form:

$$ 58 = 9 \times 6 + 4 $$

19. Final key takeaway

Euclid’s Division Lemma tells us that whenever we divide one positive integer by another positive integer, we can always write it in the form:

$$ a = bq + r $$

where the remainder is always greater than or equal to zero and smaller than the divisor.

This simple rule is the foundation for many important ideas in number theory, especially Euclid’s Division Algorithm for finding HCF.
